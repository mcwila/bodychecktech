Back Issues

Many researchers have demonstrated that classic powerlifting movements such as a deadlift have a large impact on lower back injuries. To avoid these injuries studies suggest keeping the spine in a neutral position as rounding of the back can lead to a higher risk of injury. However, in the deadlift motion, there will always be some degree of spinal flexion, or spinal rounding, as that tends to be a stronger biomechanical position for most trainees. When the lower back is rounded the hips can get closer to the bar on a horizontal axis thus reducing the torque requirement the glutes and hamstrings have to overcome, to better visualize this distance the figure below, Figure 1, shows a person deadlifting with a neutral spine and a rounded spine.

How to determine good pain vs. bad pain

It can sometimes be difficult to determine the difference between good pain (or soreness) versus bad pain. However, soreness and pain are two different feelings and the main way to tell the difference is to determine if it a dull pain or a sharp pain. A dull pain implies that it is good pain. This pain can be due to a build up of lactic acid or micro-tears in the muscles. Sudden sharp pain tends to be a sign of bad pain, especially if it significantly hinders your performance.
